About this project



Maintaining a healthy brain during one's life is the uppermost goal in pursuing health and longevity. As the population ages,
the burden of neurological disorders and challenges for the preservation of brain health increase.
It is therefore vital to understand what brain health is.
With the increase in the number of brain related disorders we present our vision to
develop an automated tool to deal with this problem where we analyze the EEG signals
generated by the brain to check sleep patterns, alcoholism and depressive behavior.

Introduction

The Electroencephalogram (EEG) signals are recorded in the classic 10-20 EEG system where electrodes
are positioned in such a way that distance between any two adjacent electrodes is between 10%
to 20% of the total front-back or left-right distance of the skull. This method helps in proper examination of the
patient as it is capable of covering a large portion of the brain with as few electrodes as possible.


The EEG readings obtained for the study are basically the voltage difference between any two electrodes.
For example, the reading Fz-Pz shall correspond to the voltage difference between the electrodes at position Fz and Pz.


The average bandpower of an EEG signal

One of the most widely used method to analyze EEG data is to decompose the signal into functionally distinct frequency bands,
such as delta (0.5-4 Hz), theta (4-8 Hz), alpha (8-12 Hz), beta (12-30 Hz), and gamma (30-100 Hz).

Brain waves:


This implies the decomposition of the EEG signal into frequency components, which is commonly achieved
through Fourier transforms. The almost invariably used algorithm to compute the Fourier transform
(and arguably the most important signal processing algorithm) is the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), which returns,
for each frequency bin, a complex number from which one can then easily extract the amplitude and phase of the signal at
that specific frequency. In spectral analysis, it is then common to take the magnitude-squared of the FFT to
obtain an estimate of the power spectral density (or power spectrum, or periodogram), expressed in (micro)-Volts per Hertz
in the case of EEG data.

Methodology

  1. Loading Data :
    2. The recordings from Sleep Physionet dataset are available in edf format. To begin computation activity on the data,
    we need to load the data into a Raw object (mne.io.Raw) containing the EEG and annotations.
  2. Filtering of Data :
    3. Most of the time, the recorded EEG Signals have some data which are beyond the extremities
    during a normal procedure. These ambiguous readings are called noise which are caused because of
    technical issues. This noise needs to be filtered out before any computations. We apply a Low-Pass Filter
    where we filter out any frequency above 30Hz.
  3. Extraction Of Epochs & Windowing:
    4. The recordings are available in 30s windows. We extract the non-overlapping epochs from the
    dataset and convert each extracted epoch as a PyTorch dataset. All these PyTorch converted
    epochs are now concatenated into a single dataset.
  4. Making the Data Ready for Models:
    5. Each data in the dataset consists of the following :-
    a) 2-D array consisting of 2 rows and 3000 columns :- The rows represent the features and the columns represent
    second-wise voltage readings.
    b) Integer values ranging from 0 to 4 :- This single value denotes the state of sleep of the patient as follows -
    1. Wakefulness
    2. Light Sleep
    3. Deeper Sleep
    4. Deep Sleep
    5. Rapid Eye Movement
    These two data are extracted into different lists.
    The first list which contains 2-D arrays cannot be directly fed to any models.
    We use statistical tools to compute the Average Power Spectrum Density to compute the average psd of each signal
    and store it in a suitable manner. Now our data is ready to be fed to different models.
